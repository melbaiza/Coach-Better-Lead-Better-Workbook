Maxwell's Four Phases of Leadership

Coaching Focus & Guiding Questions

The Coaching Phases Framework maps leaders along a Skill / Will matrix — use this to determine where each coachee is and what kind of coaching they need.
Will ↑
Phase 2
"I Know What I Don't Know"
Low Skill / High Will
Phase 4
"I Simply Go Because I Know"
High Skill / High Will
Phase 1
"I Don't Know What I Don't Know"
Low Skill / Low Will
Phase 3
"I Grow and Know, and It Starts to Show"
High Skill / Variable Will
Skill →
Phase 1 — Awareness-building
Key Questions:
• What are the most pressing challenges in your school/district?
• What outcomes are you hoping to achieve?
• What do you think are key components of effective leadership?
Phase 2 — Skill Development
Key Questions:
• What specific skills do you need to develop?
• How can you apply what you've learned in real-time situations?
• What short-term goals can you set for skill-building?
Phase 3 — Refinement & Confidence
Key Questions:
• What recent successes have you experienced?
• What advanced skills would you like to master?
• What helps you feel more confident in your leadership?
Phase 4 — Autonomy & Strategy
Key Questions:
• How are you using your autonomy to drive strategic goals?
• What innovative ideas are you considering?
• How can you delegate effectively to develop others?

Coach the Case — Work Session 1

Principal Kiki — Work Session 1: The Struggle is Real

Principal Kiki is a first-year principal at a high-need urban school. She came into the role with enormous energy and passion, but six months in, she is overwhelmed. Data is flat, two of her strongest teachers have requested transfers, and her assistant principal seems disengaged. Kiki finds herself staying until 8pm every night, making decisions alone.

In their first coaching session, Kiki struggles to articulate what she needs. She jumps between concerns without landing on any of them. She is clearly operating in crisis mode.

Mr. Elias — The Reluctant Teacher

Mr. Elias is a veteran teacher with 22 years of experience. His practice has stagnated. He rarely attends optional professional development, dismisses new initiatives as "fads," and openly questions whether coaching will help him. He shows up to their first session 10 minutes late, arms crossed.

2Section 2 — Identity

Identity & Reflection: Know Your Why

Coaching starts within.

"Education is a system; it isn't a why. A why is personal, individual."
School Leader Thought Work
  • Think back to the moment that led you to pursue leadership. What was the driving force?
  • Why do you believe your work as a school leader is important?
  • If you could focus on one aspect of your role that aligns most with your "Why", what would it be?
District Leader Thought Work
  • What inspired you to take on a leadership role within the district?
  • How do you ensure principals remain connected to their "Why"?
  • How can you model "Why-centered" leadership in your own work?
1
Why do I do the work I do?

Start with your instinct. Don't overthink it.

2
Why does that matter to me?

Dig one level deeper. What's the personal value or belief driving that answer?

3
Why is that important for others / the system / students?

Zoom out. How does your why connect to something bigger than yourself?

...so that...

3Section 3 — Coach the Case

Coach the Case — Work Session 4

Mr. Avery — The Committed Leader Who Can't Execute

Mr. Avery is a second-year assistant principal who is deeply committed to improving outcomes for students. He arrives early, stays late, participates actively in every PD session, and enthusiastically agrees with every coaching recommendation. But week after week, the agreed-upon action items never happen.

His coach is starting to wonder: Is Mr. Avery a Phase 2 leader stuck in execution? Is there a deeper barrier — fear, imposter syndrome, or perfectionism — preventing him from acting?

Coaching Challenge: Mr. Avery's barriers are not about will — they're about execution. Your coaching must shift from inspiration to accountability structures and concrete skill-building.
